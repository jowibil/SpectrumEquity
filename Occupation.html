The Role of Occupational Segregation in the Philippines

Traditional gender roles and societal expectations often push men and women into different types of jobs. In the Philippines, women are mostly found in lower-paying fields like teaching, caregiving, retail, and office work. Meanwhile, men are more likely to work in higher-paying jobs such as engineering, IT, and leadership positions. This job division adds to the gender pay gap and limits women’s chances to earn more and advance in their careers. Although 41% of STEM (Science, Technology, Engineering, and Math) graduates in the Philippines are women, only 36.6% get jobs in STEM fields within a year. Challenges like gender bias, lack of support, and family responsibilities make it harder for women to enter and stay in these careers. To reduce this gap, the country needs better career guidance for young women, more scholarships and training in STEM, fair hiring practices, and workplace policies that support both women and men equally.

Overcoming occupational segregation requires:

Multi-faceted approach that begins with challenging long-standing stereotypes about which careers are suitable for men or women. Societal norms often discourage women from pursuing roles in fields like engineering, technology, or construction, while steering men away from caregiving and teaching positions. To break these patterns, it’s important to expand access by providing women with the resources, encouragement, and support they need to explore non-traditional career paths. This includes offering scholarships, training programs, and mentorship opportunities in underrepresented fields. Additionally, employers play a key role by adopting inclusive hiring practices that promote diversity and fairness in recruitment, promotion, and workplace culture. Creating an environment where both women and men can thrive in any profession is essential to closing the gender gap and achieving true workplace equality.
